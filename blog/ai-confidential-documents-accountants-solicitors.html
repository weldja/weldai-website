Can You Use AI with Confidential Documents? What UK Accountants and Solicitors Need to Know

By James Weld May 2026 7 min read
JW
James Weld

AI document tools are genuinely useful. But for accountants, solicitors, HR consultancies, and anyone else handling confidential client information, the enthusiasm is tempered by an obvious question:

If I use AI to search my documents, where exactly does my data go?

It's the right question to ask. And the honest answer is: it depends entirely on which tool you use and how it's set up.

This guide gives you a clear, plain-English breakdown of what different AI tools actually do with your data — and what to look for if you're serious about keeping client information confidential.

The Two Fundamentally Different Approaches

Not all AI document tools work the same way. There's a crucial architectural difference between them.

Approach 1: Cloud-based AI (documents uploaded to third-party servers)

Tools like Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT file uploads, Google NotebookLM, and most SaaS AI assistants work by sending your documents to their cloud infrastructure. Your files are processed, stored, and indexed on servers you don't own or control.

These services are convenient and often well-designed. But they require you to transfer your documents outside your own infrastructure — which for professional services firms raises immediate questions about client confidentiality, data protection, and professional conduct rules.

Approach 2: Locally-hosted AI (documents never leave your server)

A locally-hosted system installs on your own server or PC. Your documents are indexed and stored entirely on your own infrastructure. When a question is asked, only the question and a few relevant paragraphs are sent to an AI model to generate the answer.

Your full documents never leave your building.

How a locally-hosted system handles a query

User asks: "What is our standard fee for a probate matter under £500,000?" → System finds the relevant clause in your fee schedule on your local server → Sends only that clause + the question to Claude AI → Returns the answer with source citation. The rest of your documents stay untouched on your server.

The Specific Risks for Regulated Professionals

Solicitors and SRA obligations

The SRA Code of Conduct requires solicitors to keep client information confidential and to consider carefully before sharing it with third parties. Uploading client documents to a cloud AI platform constitutes sharing with a third party — and the SRA's guidance on technology and AI emphasises that firms remain responsible for client data regardless of what tools they use.

For routine internal knowledge — procedures, precedents, standard clauses — cloud AI is likely low risk. For client-specific files, the calculus changes significantly.

Accountants and GDPR

Accountancy practices handle significant volumes of personal data — tax returns, payroll records, management accounts. Under UK GDPR, transferring this data to a cloud AI provider requires a lawful basis and typically a Data Processing Agreement with the provider.

Most major AI providers do offer DPAs, but you need to read them carefully. Some reserve the right to use your data to improve their models unless you opt out or use an enterprise tier.

HR consultancies

HR files contain some of the most sensitive personal data a business holds — disciplinary records, medical information, salary data. Using a cloud AI tool to search HR files without explicit DPIA assessment and staff notification could create ICO exposure.

Important: This guide provides general information, not legal advice. If you're handling regulated client data, speak to your compliance officer or data protection lead before deploying any AI tool — including a locally-hosted one.

Questions to Ask Before Using Any AI Document Tool

  • ✓Do my documents leave my own server or network at any point?
  • ✓Is there a Data Processing Agreement I can sign with the AI provider?
  • ✓Does the provider's TOS explicitly prohibit training on my data?
  • ✓Where are the provider's servers located — UK, EU, or US?
  • ✓Can I delete my data from their systems on request?
  • ✓What happens to my data if I cancel my subscription?
  • ✓Have I completed a DPIA if processing special category data?

A Practical Framework for Professional Services Firms

Based on conversations with accountants and solicitors, here's a sensible approach to deploying AI document search without creating compliance headaches:

Green light — low risk

  • Internal procedures, policies, and staff handbooks
  • Standard precedents and template documents
  • Product or service information and fee schedules
  • Regulatory guidance summaries (non-client specific)
  • Training materials and onboarding documentation

Amber — consider carefully

  • Anonymised client case studies
  • General correspondence templates
  • Financial models without identifying information

Red — take advice first

  • Client-specific correspondence and files
  • HR files containing personal or medical data
  • Tax returns and personal financial records
  • Anything subject to legal professional privilege

Starting with green-light documents gives your team the productivity benefits of AI search immediately, while you take the time to assess the compliance picture for more sensitive data.

The Bottom Line

AI document search is not inherently incompatible with client confidentiality — but the architecture matters enormously. A cloud tool that stores your documents on external servers is a fundamentally different proposition from a locally-hosted system where your documents never leave your network.

For most small professional services firms, the pragmatic starting point is clear: deploy AI for your internal knowledge first, get comfortable with how it works, and expand carefully from there.

The firms that figure out how to use AI responsibly within their compliance framework will have a real advantage over those that either avoid it entirely or adopt it without thinking through the implications.
